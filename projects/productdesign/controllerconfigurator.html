Controller Configurator Design

Concept

Windows gaming is a massive, ever-expanding space, yet native support for controller configuration and calibration is extremely limited. This project's goal is to allow one to customize the functionality of their controllers through the Windows settings. Desirable, accessible features can be seen in Valve's Steam Controller or Microsoft's Xbox Adaptive Controller but require additional software to configure.

Potential Features

Customizing joystick dead zones and remapping buttons is only the beginning of the features I envision for the configurator. Joysticks and triggers could have adjustable acceleration curves, and to accommodate inputs of any kind, custom controllers could be configured with inputs from trackballs, touch pads, dials, and sliders to even microphones, all with configurable behavior.

The Xbox Accessories app allows configurability of joystick sensitivity curves.

In addition, users could remap inputs to output muti-key macros or multiple input combinations to output alternate behavior, or even swap between different configuration profiles to allow more functionality from the same limited inputs on a controller. Outputs may also be mapped to other accessibility-improving functionality, such as a controller-compatible on-screen keyboard or speech-to-text dictation. These features could even boost productivity in professional applications, where a user may desire more shortcuts to enrich and speed up their workflow.

The Steam Controller's on-screen keyboard allows one to type very effectively with a controller with only two fingers. Predictive text options allow for even quicker typing.

Conclusions

This feature may not only have implications for gaming, but also for greater accessibility in everyday OS operation. Mouse and keyboard use may pose challenges to the differently abled, so a controller, especially a custom one, could be an easier to use, fully functioned primary input device. One might even configure secondary, tertiary, or even more additional input devices to further enhance the efficiency and versatility of their Windows use.

Enabling controllers to be used as primary inputs has great implications for accessibility, including the Xbox Adaptive Controller for near infinite customizability, as well as for Windows on handheld devices, such as the Steam deck.

Handheld gaming devices such as Valve's Steam Deck may also benefit from a Windows controller configurator. Running Windows on such a device would allow wider game compatibility, as well as enable full-fledged computer functionality and synchronicity with other Windows devices.
